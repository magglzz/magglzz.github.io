Partner Portal

Partner portal is a product that connects us to our partners, and our partners to our consumers. Through portal, partners can update their community information, report move ins, and manage their referrals.

Question

How can we improve the experience for our partners?

Goals

Our goal was to create a product that would build a better relationship with our partners. This included making it easier for users to report move-ins, putting a system in place for tracking leads and reporting, and converting most operations to self-serve.

The Process

Dissecting the Problem

For more context, read a bit about Company XYZ

I was given a set of business goals that I translated to better human experiences. Focusing on these human experiences is like the northern light for my design process. For me, that starts with getting to know the people that will be using our product. I sat with the PM and had casual conversations with some users to just get a feel of how they felt about Partner Portal and how it tied in with their job and what motivates them. In our initial conversations, we focused on the big picture. We let this be the foundation for our product and later dug deeper to add the details. I translated these conversations to storyboards to discover what steps could be taken out, what felt natural and what didn’t, and opportunities where we could delight the user.

Scribbling out ideas and flows helps me to think - no matter how incomprehensible it may seem.

From ideation to creation

The original portal design had little thought regarding the flows, visual hierarchy, or design patterns. For instance, the most important feature--reporting a move-in and viewing your referrals--was buried under five steps and categorized under “Community Performance,” which partners did not recognize as relating to referrals or move ins.

Portal move-ins page circa 2015. If you don't know the name of your referral, it could take a lot of scrolling to find. There was also a lot of confusion between the Finalize Reporting button and Report Move-in button.

Problems

  • Unable to update rates in real time, 24 hour lag
  • Impossible to find leads in database
  • Can’t send or see updates about a lead
  • Move-in reporting is confusing

Flow Mapping

I focused most of the IA on our main flow; reporting a move in. Which parts could we cut? Which parts were more complex? Are there edge cases? Are there opportunities that we missed? The organization of the system was designed to accommodate new features that could be added in the future without compromising the most important ones.

Modular Layout

One of best ways to ensure a move-in is with up-to-date information. The more accurate the information we have about a community, the better we can match them with a consumer. Historically, communities had only been able to manage their rate information in the Portal. We decided to significantly expand that functionality in the new Portal by adding the capacity for partners to set and update not just rates, but also images, promotions, and contact information.

The first iteration had a table system that limited the program to a horizontal space and didn’t support images.

Where the images at?

I approached the product team and argued for an overhaul of the entire design in favor of a new one that would support current and new features. The resulting layout has a modular UI with flexible containers to hold varying amounts of information, allowed room for additional features, and can easily be made responsive.

Visual organization = mental peace

Prioritizing Flows

We had a set number of engineering points allocated for this story and that meant we had to prioritize and decrease scope. We focused on our initial goal of building a better relationship with our partners and used our research to discover what might be the biggest driving factor. In the end, we decided on re-designing the Report Move-ins Flow.

I started out with a critique of the current flow. Here is one page of many notes:

My notes on the old referral details page.

The first version solved many of the issues we had with the old portal, but further testing revealed that it still wasn’t very intuitive. More move-ins were being reported, but when we followed up on them, only the same amount were valid. It seemed that people were accidentally reporting move-ins.

The two places you can enter the flow from are the Referrals page and the Referral Details page. The split here was to reflect the two different ways you could report a move in. You could either report it on a monthly basis, in which you would select all the move ins you had that month and report them, or on a case by case basis, in which you would report them one by one as they moved in. Chains liked to use the monthly reporting feature, and SMBs used the case by case feature.

Diagram of report move in flow

So What Went Wrong?

First of all, the Create a Move-in button threw people off. To me, the flow was the same thing whether reporting monthly or case by case, but to the user they were very different and the buttons didn't show that.

On the left is the Referral page (monthly reporting) and on the right is the Referral Details page (case by case reporting).

Having the move-in register in our system before the user hit the final submit button was a decision we made based on fear. Fear that the user wouldn't know to follow through on the flow to submit the move-in. Boy were we wrong. In fact, the user would accidentally click report move-in so often that they were reporting move-ins that didn't happen, causing more work for both sides.

The Enter Costs and Data page is optional information and a user can submit a move in without this data. Because of this, we register the move in as reported before they get to this page, but the flow implies that it would not be reported until they hit the final submit.

In the case where a community had no move-ins for the month, they needed a way to report that as well. However, the only way to do that was by entering the Report Flow and selecting Report No Move-ins at the first step. This was counterintuitive and caused many people to get frustrated.

Users have to click the create move in button in order to enter the move in flow and report no move ins, which made no sense.

Iterate, iterate, iterate!

To solve the above problems, I developed a new flow that could be used for monthly and case by case reporting. When the user comes to the page to report a move-in, they can do so directly on the page without ever having to navigate away. The seamlessness of the new flow made it just as easy to report mulitple move-ins as it was to report just one. We took it a step further and made it even easier to report by ordering the referrals on the page based on their likelihood of move in.

Instead of having to enter a flow, users can just do a quick interaction on each row of data to report a move in.

I realized that communities only report no move ins at the end of the month, so I designed the system to detect this and notify the user with a blue info bar whenever they log in within 1 week of when reporting is due. Communities with no move-ins can report that by clicking the link in the blue bar.

The user no longer has to enter the create move in flow to report no move ins.

The area also served as a great place to list out referrals they had already reported on a case by case basis for the month. If those were the only ones they had to report, we gave them an easy way to finalize their reporting and stop getting emails from us.

The second state of the blue bar is if there are reported move ins for the month. In this case, we remind them what they are and let them finalize their reporting.

In the new flow, the move-in would only register in our system if the user completed the reporting process. Doing so prevented duplicate and accidental reporting. Additionally, placing the optional tag next to the form labels instead of in copy was a more concise and noticeable way to inform users that they could click "Next" without the extra information. This eliminated any ambuiguities about whether they could continue reporting or not.

Right next to the "Report Move-in" button on each row is also a "More" link to report other statuses for the lead besides "Moved-in". This placement made sense for the user and was a benefit to us since we could get extra information at little added cost to the user.

In the end, we were able to cut the number of steps in monthly reporting down to 2 and maintain consistency between monthly and case by case reporting. Making the system dynamic to catch users whenever reporting was relevant was also helpful in getting more valid reports and keeping the user engaged with our business. We saw a significant increase in the number of users reporting their move ins through portal versus by emailing their account managers, and a much higher move in rate from the communities that saw the most value in the product.

What I Learned

Portal was one of the first big projects I worked on in my time here and it had a lot of eyes on it. Design is much more than just creating flows and pretty visuals, it's also about collaboration and communication. Managing expectations with stakeholders and working with them every step of the way, made them feel personally invested in my designs and when something didn't work the way we expected it to, blame was not passed around. Instead, there was a collective effort to reinvent and improve.
